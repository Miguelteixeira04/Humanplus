THE TECHNOLOGICAL REVOLUTION

HISTORY / ORIGINS +
1950

THE TURING TEST

It all began with a simple question: "Can machines think?" In 1950, Alan Turing published his seminal paper "Computing Machinery and Intelligence," introducing what is now known as the Turing Test [24].

1956

BIRTH OF AI

The field was officially born at the historic Dartmouth Summer Research Project. Organized by John McCarthy and colleagues, this workshop formally coined the term "Artificial Intelligence" [25].

2012

DEEP LEARNING

The landscape changed with Deep Learning and the ImageNet classification victory [7]. Ponti (2017) explains how these neural networks function to perceive the world, driven by data and processing power [6].

2023+

GENERATIVE ERA

We have moved from systems that classify data to systems that create it. Modern AI models are now capable of complex tasks, as detailed in the Data Science Academy resources [29].

DOC_02

MORAL FOUNDATIONS

ETHICS / PHILOSOPHY +

MORAL PHILOSOPHY AS A FOUNDATION FOR ETHICS

Despite the novelty of digital technologies, the ethical questions they raise are deeply rooted in classical moral philosophy. Consequentialism and deontological ethics remain indispens able lenses through which contemporary technological practices can be examined. Utilitarianism, as articulated by Jeremy Bentham, proposed that moral action should be evaluated according to its capacity to maximize happiness.

HUMAN ETHICS IN A DIGITAL CIVILIZATION

Ethics has always emerged at moments when human practices out pace existing moral frameworks. From Aristotle’s reflections on virtue in the polis to Kant’s rational moral law in the Enlightenment, ethical philosophy has historically responded to transformations in how humans live, act, and exercise power.

FROM MORAL AGENTS TO MORAL SYSTEMS

Traditional ethical theory assumes that moral agents exist as distinct, autonomous individuals, possessing the ability to intend, deliberate, and reflect morally. Moral responsibility, in classical frameworks, is grounded in the assumption that agents understand the consequences of their actions and can therefore be praised or blamed accordingly. Digital systems fundamentally disrupt this assumption.

DOC_03

ETHICS IN AI – WHEN MACHINES MAKE DECISIONS

BIAS / DISCRIMINATION +

ETHICAL ISSUES

Allocating responsibility in complex AI systems remains one of the greatest ethical and legal challenges today (Floridi, 2024). Many deep learning algorithms operate as “black boxes,”producing results without it being fully understandable how they reached those conclusions (Müller, 2020).

REAL CASES OF ALGORITHMIC DISCRIMINATION

Case 1: Amazon In 2014, Amazon developed an experimental AI-based recruitment tool. By 2015, it was discovered that the system was biased against women, leading the company to abandon the project in early 2018 (Amazon AI recruitment tool, 2018).

Case 2: Apple Card In 2019, the Apple Card became the subject of gender discrimination allegations in credit approval (Apple Card gender discrimination, 2019). Several users, including tech entrepreneur David Heinemeier Hansson, reported that the algorithm offered significantly higher credit limits to men than to women with identical or superior financial profiles.

Case 3: Financial Sector Studies indicates that the algorithms banks use for loan and credit approvals can disproportionately disadvantage racial minorities, resulting in higher rejection rates compared to white applicants with comparable financial profiles (Bartlett, Morse, Stanton, & Wallace, 2019).

THE ROOT OF THE PROBLEM

These cases illustrate a fundamental principle: algorithms are not neutral. They reflect the data on which they were trained, and that data inevitably contains the biases, inequalities, and injustices of the society that produced it. Far from being objective arbiters, algorithmic systems encode historical patterns of exclusion and power relations, often rendering them invisible behind a façade of technical efficiency.

ARTIFICIAL MORAL AGENCY AND MACHINE ETHICS

Müller (2020) examines the concept of AI systems as moral agents. The field of machine ethics investigates the potential for creating autonomous systems capable of genuine moral reasoning. Some researchers, including proponents of “technogenic morality,” suggest that AI could potentially develop a “seventh stage” of moral development, prioritizing the unconditional preservation of life over legalism and obedience in extreme ethical dilemmas.

GOVERNANCE AND REGULATION OF AI

LAW / POLICY +

PRINCIPLES FOR SOCIETY

To manage these risks, Floridi et al. (2018) proposed a "Unified Framework of Five Principles for AI in Society" [2]. This framework seeks to align technological innovation with human rights and democratic values, forming the basis for governance.

HUMAN RIGHTS AND FOUNDATIONS OF AI GOVERNANCE

AI governance is a multidisciplinary concept encompassing technical risk management, ethical principles, legal regulation, institutional oversight, and accountability mechanisms. Rather than referring to a single regulatory instrument, governance denotes an ecosystem of norms, practices, and institutions that shape how AI systems are designed, deployed, and monitored

ACCOUNTABILITY IN ALGORITHMIC SYSTEMS

Accountability is one of the most contested dimensions of AI governance. As AI systems increasingly influence decisions in finance, healthcare, employment, and public administration, determining who is responsible for harmful outcomes becomes increasingly complex. Rumlus (2025) emphasizes that algorithmic accountability should be understood not merely as a legal obligation but as a structural process.

THE EUROPEAN UNION’S REGULATORY MODEL

Among global actors, the European Union has developed the most comprehensive and legally binding framework for AI governance. Hulok (2025) characterizes the EU model as one that integrates ethics, fundamental rights, and enforceable legal instruments.

GAPS AND THE IMPLEMENTATION CHALLENG

While governance models diverge, areas of partial convergence are emerging. The Anatomy of AI Policies (2026) identifies growing consensusaround the need for safety standards, data governance, and public–private cooperation. However, convergence at the level of principles does not translate into convergence in enforcement. Implementation gaps remain a critical weakness of global AI governance.

REFS: [08] Angwin (2016) | [09] Dastin (2018) | [11] Portal IDP (2024) | [13] UFSM (2024)

IMPACTS OF AI ON SOCIETY AND LABOUR MARKETS

WORKFORCE / SOCIETY +

TRANSFORMING WORK AND LABOUR MARKETS

Artificial intelligence is accelerating automation across a wide range of economic sectors. Unlike previous technological shifts that primarily affected manual or routine labour, AI-driven systems increasingly automate cognitive tasks such as data analysis, pattern recognition, language processing, and decision support (Autor, Levy, & Murnane, 2003).

ALGORITHMIC POWER AT WORK

Beyond automation, AI reshapes labour through algorithmic management. In platform-based and logistics-driven industries, algorithms assign tasks, evaluate performance, set wages, and enforce disciplinary measures (Rosenblat & Stark, 2016). Workers are increasingly managed by systems that operate continuously, at scale, and often without transparency.

REFS: [20] Hinton (2025) | [22] Gebru (2024) | [23] Gebru (2021)

CRITICS – EXPERTS WARN ABOUT THE RISKS OF AI

TURING AWARD // PIONEER

YOSHUA BENGIO

“Efforts to build AI systems designed to act in their own interest should be criminalized.”

As one of the most outspoken voices regarding the existential risks of artificial intelligence, Yoshua Bengio, Turing Award winner and Deep Learning pioneer, has consistently warned about uncontrolled AI development. In an interview with Jacobin Brasil (2024), he stated that “efforts to build AI systems designed to act in their own interest and potentially dominate humans should be criminalized” (Bengio, 2024, as cited in Jacobin Brasil, 2024).

KEY PROPOSAL: International treaties to ban the development of autonomous agents capable of self-replication.
GODFATHER OF AI

GEOFFREY HINTON

“It is quite conceivable that humanity is just a passing phase in the evolution of intelligence.”

Concerns about the dangers of AI have led Geoffrey Hinton, often called the “Godfather of AI” and also a Turing Award winner, to leave his position at Google in 2023 to speak freely on the subject.

KEY PROPOSAL: Immediate global pause on giant experiments and regulation of military AI.
ETHICS SPECIALIST

TIMNIT GEBRU

“The danger is not that AI will destroy us, but that it will be used to control us.”

The debate over social justice and AI has been significantly shaped by Timnit Gebru, an AI ethics specialist who gained prominence after her controversial dismissal from Google in 2020. She was fired after questioning company practices related to diversity and ethics in AI systems (Nota Alta ESPM, 2024).

KEY PROPOSAL: Focus on labor rights, stopping data exploitation, and preventing algorithmic bias.
