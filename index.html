THE TECHNOLOGICAL REVOLUTION OF THE 21st CENTURY

HISTORY / ORIGINS +
1950

THE TURING TEST

It all began with a simple question: "Can machines think?" In 1950, Alan Turing published his seminal paper "Computing Machinery and Intelligence," introducing what is now known as the Turing Test (Turing, 1950).

1956

BIRTH OF AI

The field was officially born at the historic Dartmouth Summer Research Project. Organized by John McCarthy and colleagues, this workshop formally coined the term "Artificial Intelligence" (McCarthy et al., 1956).

2012

DEEP LEARNING

The landscape changed with Deep Learning. As outlined by Goodfellow, Bengio, and Courville, deep learning architectures overcame many representational limitations, allowing AI systems to capture hierarchical and abstract features of data (Goodfellow, Bengio, & Courville, 2016).

2023+

GENERATIVE ERA

We have moved to systems that create data. The introduction of the transformer architecture and Large Language Models (LLMs) like GPT series signifies a shift towards general and flexible AI systems (Vaswani et al., 2017).

DOC_02

ETHICS – MORAL FOUNDATIONS IN THE DIGITAL AGE

ETHICS / PHILOSOPHY +

MORAL PHILOSOPHY AS A FOUNDATION FOR ETHICS

Despite the novelty of digital technologies, the ethical questions they raise are deeply rooted in classical moral philosophy. Consequentialism and deontological ethics remain indispensable lenses through which contemporary technological practices can be examined. Utilitarianism, as articulated by Jeremy Bentham, proposed that moral action should be evaluated according to its capacity to maximize happiness. Bentham’s assertion that “the greatest happiness of the greatest number is the foundation of morals and legislation” (Bentham, 1789) resonates strongly with contemporary narratives of technological optimization. Efficiency, scalability, and aggregate benefit are frequently invoked to justify algorithmic systems that operate across populations rather than individuals.

HUMAN ETHICS IN A DIGITAL CIVILIZATION

Ethics has always emerged at moments when human practices outpace existing moral frameworks. From Aristotle’s reflections on virtue in the polis to Kant’s rational moral law in the Enlightenment, ethical philosophy has historically responded to transformations in how humans live, act, and exercise power. The digital age represents one of the most profound such transformations. Artificial intelligence, data-driven systems, and algorithmic governance do not merely assist human decision-making, they increasingly mediate it, reshape it, and in some cases replace it.

FROM MORAL AGENTS TO MORAL SYSTEMS

Traditional ethical theory assumes that moral agents exist as distinct, autonomous individuals, possessing the ability to intend, deliberate, and reflect morally. Moral responsibility, in classical frameworks, is grounded in the assumption that agents understand the consequences of their actions and can therefore be praised or blamed accordingly. Digital systems fundamentally disrupt this assumption. Algorithmic decisions rarely originate from a single locus of agency, instead, they emerge from complex interactions among data sets, statistical models, technical infrastructures, institutional policies, and economic incentives. Within such environments, responsibility becomes distributed, fragmented, and increasingly difficult to assign.

DOC_03

ETHICS IN AI – WHEN MACHINES MAKE DECISIONS

BIAS / DISCRIMINATION +

ETHICAL ISSUES

Luciano Floridi (2024) identifies four fundamental ethical issues exacerbated by AI systems: accountability, transparency, bias, and privacy. When an AI system makes a decision that results in harm, whether a misdiagnosis, an autonomous vehicle accident, or an unjust judicial ruling.Allocating responsibility in complex AI systems remains one of the greatest ethical and legal challenges today (Floridi, 2024). Many deep learning algorithms operate as “black boxes,”producing results without it being fully understandable how they reached those conclusions (Müller, 2020).

REAL CASES OF ALGORITHMIC DISCRIMINATION

Case 1: Amazon

In 2014, Amazon developed an experimental AI-based recruitment tool. By 2015, it was discovered that the system was biased against women, leading the company to abandon the project in early 2018 (Amazon AI recruitment tool, 2018).The machine learning system was trained on a decade of resumes submitted to the company, predominantly from men, reflecting the historical composition of the tech industry.

Case 2: Apple Card

In 2019, the Apple Card became the subject of gender discrimination allegations in credit approval (Apple Card gender discrimination, 2019). Several users, including tech entrepreneur David Heinemeier Hansson, reported that the algorithm offered significantly higher credit limits to men than to women with identical or superior financial profiles. The issue drew public attention after Hansson posted screenshots of his account, showing a credit limit twenty times higher than that of his wife, despite her having a stronger credit history.

Case 3: Financial Sector

Studies indicates that the algorithms banks use for loan and credit approvals can disproportionately disadvantage racial minorities, resulting in higher rejection rates compared to white applicants with comparable financial profiles (Bartlett, Morse, Stanton, & Wallace, 2019). This algorithmic discrimination perpetuates historical economic inequalities and limits opportunities for social mobility, effectively reinforcing patterns of systemic bias that have persisted for decades.

THE ROOT OF THE PROBLEM

These cases illustrate a fundamental principle: algorithms are not neutral. They reflect the data on which they were trained, and that data inevitably contains the biases, inequalities, and injustices of the society that produced it. Far from being objective arbiters, algorithmic systems encode historical patterns of exclusion and power relations, often rendering them invisible behind a façade of technical efficiency.Brazilian research on algorithmic discrimination emphasizes that we are still at an early stage in understanding and addressing these challenges, particularly from the perspectives of human rights and administrative law (Silva & Oliveira, 2024). This literature highlights how algorithmic decision-making can conflict with constitutional principles such as equality before the law, due process, and transparency, especially when public authorities adopt automated systems without adequates safeguards.

ARTIFICIAL MORAL AGENCY AND MACHINE ETHICS

Müller (2020) examines the concept of AI systems as moral agents. The field of machine ethics investigates the potential for creating autonomous systems capable of genuine moral reasoning. Some researchers, including proponents of “technogenic morality,” suggest that AI could potentially develop a “seventh stage” of moral development, prioritizing the unconditional preservation of life over legalism and obedience in extreme ethical dilemmas. Kantian philosophy provides a particularly relevant framework for this discussion. Concepts from Kantian ethics, such as the categorical imperative, autonomy of the will, and human dignity, offer important guidance for designing autonomous AI systems that act morally (Kantian Ethics Applied to AI, 2024). Integrating these philosophical principles can help ensure that AI systems make ethically sound decisions while respecting human values and dignity.

DOC_04

GOVERNANCE AND REGULATION OF AI

LAW / POLICY +

HUMAN RIGHTS AND FOUNDATIONS OF AI GOVERNANCE

AI governance is a multidisciplinary concept encompassing technical risk management, ethical principles, legal regulation, institutional oversight, and accountability mechanisms. Rather than referring to a single regulatory instrument, governance denotes an ecosystem of norms, practices, and institutions that shape how AI systems are designed, deployed, and monitored. A systematic review by Batool, Zowghi, and Bano (2025) shows that the literature does not converge on a single definition of AI governance. Instead, it identifies recurring dimensions such as transparency, fairness, human oversight, safety, traceability, and accountability. Importantly, governance is not limited to state actors: private companies, standards-setting organizations, civil society, and users all participate in shaping governance outcomes.

ACCOUNTABILITY IN ALGORITHMIC SYSTEMS

Accountability is one of the most contested dimensions of AI governance. As AI systems increasingly influence decisions in finance, healthcare, employment, and public administration, determining who is responsible for harmful outcomes becomes increasingly complex. Rumlus (2025) emphasizes that algorithmic accountability should be understood not merely as a legal obligation but as a structural process. Effective accountability requires transparency mechanisms, auditability protocols, institutional oversight, and avenues for redress. When accountability is weak or absent, power asymmetries between AI developers and affected individuals widen, eroding public trust. AI systems distribute decision-making across datasets, models, infrastructures, and organizational incentives.

THE EUROPEAN UNION’S REGULATORY MODEL

Among global actors, the European Union has developed the most comprehensive and legally binding framework for AI governance. Hulok (2025) characterizes the EU model as one that integrates ethics, fundamental rights, and enforceable legal instruments. Rather than relying on voluntary guidelines or industry self-regulation, the EU treats AI systems as socio-technical systems capable of producing legal and societal harms. This framing justifies binding regulatory intervention. The EU’s approach is anchored in existing legal traditions, including data protection law and the Charter of Fundamental Rights. A defining feature of this model is the risk-based logic of the Artificial Intelligence Act (AI Act), which classifies AI systems according to the level of risk they pose and assigns corresponding obligations. These include transparency requirements, human oversight, quality management systems, and data governance standards for high-risk applications European AI governance increasingly incorporates explicit human rights considerations.

GAPS AND THE IMPLEMENTATION CHALLENGE

While governance models diverge, areas of partial convergence are emerging. The Anatomy of AI Policies (2026) identifies growing consensusaround the need for safety standards, data governance, and public–private cooperation. However, convergence at the level of principles does not translate into convergence in enforcement. Implementation gaps remain a critical weakness of global AI governance. Differences in legal systems, institutional capacity, and political priorities limit the effectiveness of shared norms. Without interoperable regulatory frameworks and enforcement mechanisms, global coordination remains aspirational. A persistent governance challenge concerns the opacity of complex AI systems. Many machine learning models, particularly deep learning architectures, function as black boxes, limiting interpretability.

DOC_05

IMPACTS OF AI ON SOCIETY AND LABOUR MARKETS

WORKFORCE / SOCIETY +

TRANSFORMING WORK AND LABOUR MARKETS

Artificial intelligence is accelerating automation across a wide range of economic sectors. Unlike previous technological shifts that primarily affected manual or routine labour, AI-driven systems increasingly automate cognitive tasks such as data analysis, pattern recognition, language processing, and decision support (Autor, Levy, & Murnane, 2003). As a result, professions once considered resilient to automation law, finance, medicine, and education are undergoing structural transformation. Rather than eliminating entire occupations, AI often reconfigures tasks within jobs. Research shows that occupations consist of bundles of tasks, some of which are automatable while others remain dependent on human judgment, social interaction, or ethical reasoning (Acemoglu & Restrepo, 2020). This task-based transformation offers productivity gains but also alters skill requirements and employment relations

ALGORITHMIC POWER AT WORK

Beyond automation, AI reshapes labour through algorithmic management. In platform-based and logistics-driven industries, algorithms assign tasks, evaluate performance, set wages, and enforce disciplinary measures (Rosenblat & Stark, 2016). Workers are increasingly managed by systems that operate continuously, at scale, and often without transparency. Algorithmic management redistributes power within organisations. Decision-making authority shifts from human supervisors to opaque technical systems, limiting workers’ ability to contest evaluations or negotiate working conditions. This transformation has been associated with intensified surveillance, increased work pace, and psychological stress (Kellogg, Valentine, & Christin, 2020). As a result, questions of fairness, accountability, and workers’ rights have become central in discussions of AI governance. There is growing consensus that regulatory frameworks and organizational policies must be adapted to ensure transparency, enable contestation, and protect labour welfare in an increasingly automated workplace.

CRITICS – EXPERTS WARN ABOUT THE RISKS OF AI

TURING AWARD // PIONEER

YOSHUA BENGIO

“Efforts to build AI systems designed to act in their own interest should be criminalized.”

As one of the most outspoken voices regarding the existential risks of artificial intelligence, Yoshua Bengio, Turing Award winner and Deep Learning pioneer, has consistently warned about uncontrolled AI development. In an interview with Jacobin Brasil (2024), he stated that “efforts to build AI systems designed to act in their own interest and potentially dominate humans should be criminalized” (Bengio, 2024, as cited in Jacobin Brasil, 2024).

KEY PROPOSAL: Criminalization of AI systems designed to act in their own interest.
GODFATHER OF AI

GEOFFREY HINTON

“Humanity is running out of time to prepare for machines that surpass human intelligence.”

Concerns about the dangers of AI have led Geoffrey Hinton, often called the “Godfather of AI”, to leave Google in 2023. In interviews with CBS News (2025), he updated previous predictions, noting that AGI could arrive within a decade. He warned that "humanity is running out of time" and advocated for AI that genuinely cares about humans (Hinton, 2025, as cited in CBS News, 2025).

KEY PROPOSAL: Immediate global pause and preparation for superintelligent machines.
ETHICS SPECIALIST

TIMNIT GEBRU

“AI systems are fundamentally 'stochastic parrots' repeating statistical patterns.”

Timnit Gebru gained prominence after her controversial dismissal from Google in 2020, following questions about diversity and ethics (Nota Alta ESPM, 2024). Her influential work, "Stochastic Parrots" (2021), argues that large language models repeat statistical patterns without genuine understanding, potentially perpetuating biases and social inequalities (Gebru et al., 2021).

KEY PROPOSAL: Focus on halting data exploitation and preventing algorithmic bias.
