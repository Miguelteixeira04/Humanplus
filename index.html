DOC_01

THE TECHNOLOGICAL REVOLUTION

HISTORY / ORIGINS +
1950

THE TURING TEST

It all began with a simple question: "Can machines think?" In 1950, Alan Turing published his seminal paper "Computing Machinery and Intelligence," introducing what is now known as the Turing Test (Turing, 1950).

1956

BIRTH OF AI

The field was officially born at the historic Dartmouth Summer Research Project. Organized by John McCarthy and colleagues, this workshop formally coined the term "Artificial Intelligence" (McCarthy et al., 1956).

2012

DEEP LEARNING

The landscape changed with Deep Learning. As outlined by Goodfellow, Bengio, and Courville, deep learning architectures overcame many representational limitations, allowing AI systems to capture hierarchical and abstract features of data (Goodfellow, Bengio, & Courville, 2016).

2023+

GENERATIVE ERA

We have moved to systems that create data. The introduction of the transformer architecture and Large Language Models (LLMs) like GPT series signifies a shift towards general and flexible AI systems (Vaswani et al., 2017).

REFS: Turing (1950) | McCarthy et al. (1956) | Goodfellow et al. (2016) | Vaswani et al. (2017)
DOC_02

MORAL FOUNDATIONS

ETHICS / PHILOSOPHY +

MORAL PHILOSOPHY AS A FOUNDATION FOR ETHICS

Despite the novelty of digital technologies, the ethical questions they raise are deeply rooted in classical moral philosophy. Utilitarianism, as articulated by Jeremy Bentham, proposed that moral action should be evaluated according to its capacity to maximize happiness (Bentham, 1789).

HUMAN ETHICS IN A DIGITAL CIVILIZATION

From Aristotle’s reflections on virtue to Kant’s rational moral law, ethical philosophy has historically responded to transformations. Kant's categorical imperative insists that moral actions must respect rational agents as ends in themselves (Kant, 1785).

FROM MORAL AGENTS TO MORAL SYSTEMS

Traditional ethical theory assumes that moral agents exist as distinct, autonomous individuals. Digital systems fundamentally disrupt this assumption. Responsibility becomes distributed among data sets, models, and institutions (Floridi, 2016).

REFS: Bentham (1789) | Kant (1785) | Floridi (2016)
DOC_03

ETHICS IN AI – WHEN MACHINES MAKE DECISIONS

BIAS / DISCRIMINATION +

ETHICAL ISSUES

Allocating responsibility in complex AI systems remains one of the greatest challenges (Floridi, 2024). Many deep learning algorithms operate as “black boxes,” producing results without it being fully understandable how they reached those conclusions (Müller, 2020).

REAL CASES OF ALGORITHMIC DISCRIMINATION

Case 1: Amazon In 2014, Amazon developed a recruitment tool that was biased against women (Amazon AI recruitment tool, 2018).

Case 2: Apple Card In 2019, the Apple Card became the subject of gender discrimination allegations, offering higher credit limits to men than women (Apple Card gender discrimination, 2019).

THE ROOT OF THE PROBLEM

As Noble (2018) argues, algorithmic systems often reproduce "oppression by design," embedding racial, gendered, and class-based hierarchies into digital infrastructures.

REFS: Floridi (2024) | Müller (2020) | Noble (2018) | Bartlett et al. (2019)
DOC_04

GOVERNANCE AND REGULATION OF AI

LAW / POLICY +

PRINCIPLES FOR SOCIETY

Floridi et al. (2018) proposed a "Unified Framework of Five Principles for AI in Society" to align technological innovation with human rights and democratic values.

HUMAN RIGHTS AND FOUNDATIONS OF AI GOVERNANCE

Hogan and Lasek-Markey (2024) argue that ethical governance must go beyond aspirational principles and embed rights protection into enforceable regulatory frameworks.

THE EUROPEAN UNION’S REGULATORY MODEL

Hulok (2025) characterizes the EU model as one that integrates ethics, fundamental rights, and enforceable legal instruments, anchored in the AI Act.

REFS: Floridi et al. (2018) | Hogan & Lasek-Markey (2024) | Rumlus (2025) | Hulok (2025)
DOC_05

IMPACTS OF AI ON SOCIETY AND LABOUR MARKETS

WORKFORCE / SOCIETY +

TRANSFORMING WORK AND LABOUR MARKETS

AI-driven systems increasingly automate cognitive tasks. Acemoglu & Restrepo (2020) note that AI does not eliminate jobs wholesale, but reorganizes the tasks that constitute work.

ALGORITHMIC POWER AT WORK

In platform-based industries, algorithms assign tasks and evaluate performance. This has been associated with intensified surveillance and increased work pace (Kellogg, Valentine, & Christin, 2020).

REFS: Acemoglu & Restrepo (2020) | Rosenblat & Stark (2016) | Kellogg et al. (2020)

CRITICS – EXPERTS WARN ABOUT THE RISKS OF AI

TURING AWARD // PIONEER

YOSHUA BENGIO

“Efforts to build AI systems designed to act in their own interest should be criminalized.”

As one of the most outspoken voices regarding the existential risks of artificial intelligence, Yoshua Bengio, Turing Award winner and Deep Learning pioneer, has consistently warned about uncontrolled AI development. In an interview with Jacobin Brasil (2024), he stated that “efforts to build AI systems designed to act in their own interest and potentially dominate humans should be criminalized” (Bengio, 2024, as cited in Jacobin Brasil, 2024).

KEY PROPOSAL: Criminalization of AI systems designed to act in their own interest.
GODFATHER OF AI

GEOFFREY HINTON

“Humanity is running out of time to prepare for machines that surpass human intelligence.”

Concerns about the dangers of AI have led Geoffrey Hinton, often called the “Godfather of AI”, to leave Google in 2023. In interviews with CBS News (2025), he updated previous predictions, noting that AGI could arrive within a decade. He warned that "humanity is running out of time" and advocated for AI that genuinely cares about humans (Hinton, 2025, as cited in CBS News, 2025).

KEY PROPOSAL: Immediate global pause and preparation for superintelligent machines.
ETHICS SPECIALIST

TIMNIT GEBRU

“AI systems are fundamentally 'stochastic parrots' repeating statistical patterns.”

Timnit Gebru gained prominence after her controversial dismissal from Google in 2020, following questions about diversity and ethics (Nota Alta ESPM, 2024). Her influential work, "Stochastic Parrots" (2021), argues that large language models repeat statistical patterns without genuine understanding, potentially perpetuating biases and social inequalities (Gebru et al., 2021).

KEY PROPOSAL: Focus on halting data exploitation and preventing algorithmic bias.
