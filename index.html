DOC_01

THE TECHNOLOGICAL REVOLUTION

HISTORY / ORIGINS +
1950

THE TURING TEST

It all began with a simple question: "Can machines think?" In 1950, Alan Turing published his seminal paper "Computing Machinery and Intelligence," introducing what is now known as the Turing Test [24].

1956

BIRTH OF AI

The field was officially born at the historic Dartmouth Summer Research Project. Organized by John McCarthy and colleagues, this workshop formally coined the term "Artificial Intelligence" [25].

2012

DEEP LEARNING

The landscape changed with Deep Learning and the ImageNet classification victory [7]. Ponti (2017) explains how these neural networks function to perceive the world, driven by data and processing power [6].

2023+

GENERATIVE ERA

We have moved from systems that classify data to systems that create it. Modern AI models are now capable of complex tasks, as detailed in the Data Science Academy resources [29].

REFS: [06] Ponti (2017) | [07] Krizhevsky et al. (2012) | [24] Turing (1950) | [25] McCarthy et al. (1956) | [29] DSA
DOC_02

MORAL FOUNDATIONS

ETHICS / PHILOSOPHY +

CLASSICAL FRAMEWORKS

Digital ethics is rooted in classical philosophy. Branford et al. (2024) explore how The Philosophy and Ethics of AI must revisit ancient debates to address modern technological practices [3]. The challenge is applying human-centric frameworks to autonomous code.

ETHICS OF ROBOTICS

Luciano Floridi argues that we face The Ethics of Artificial Intelligence where technology creates new moral dilemmas [1]. Müller (2020) further expands on this in the "Ethics of AI and Robotics," questioning the moral agency of machines [4].

REFS: [01] Floridi (2024) | [03] Branford et al. (2024) | [04] Müller (2020)
DOC_03

WHEN MACHINES DECIDE

BIAS / DISCRIMINATION +

THE BLACK BOX PROBLEM

A critical issue is opacity. The demand for "Explainable Artificial Intelligence" is growing, as discussed by UFSM (2024), to ensure that automated decisions can be understood by humans [13]. Without explanation, accountability is impossible.

REAL WORLD BIAS

Case Study: Amazon: In 2018, Reuters reported that Amazon scrapped a secret AI recruiting tool because it was biased against women [9].

Machine Bias: Angwin (2016) highlighted how algorithms can be biased against minorities [8]. In Brazil, the discussion on "Algorithmic Discrimination" is becoming central to legal debates [11].

REFS: [08] Angwin (2016) | [09] Dastin (2018) | [11] Portal IDP (2024) | [13] UFSM (2024)
DOC_04

GOVERNANCE & REGULATION

LAW / POLICY +

PRINCIPLES FOR SOCIETY

To manage these risks, Floridi et al. (2018) proposed a "Unified Framework of Five Principles for AI in Society" [2]. This framework seeks to align technological innovation with human rights and democratic values, forming the basis for governance.

THE NEED FOR CONTROL

The lack of regulation poses existential risks. Yoshua Bengio questions, "Can humanity survive AI?" (Jacobin, 2024), arguing for strict governance to prevent the loss of control over autonomous systems [19].

REFS: [02] Floridi et al. (2018) | [19] Bengio (2024)
DOC_05

SOCIETY & LABOUR

WORKFORCE / SOCIETY +

DYSTOPIAN FUTURES?

The impact on society goes beyond efficiency. Timnit Gebru warns that "AI causes fear of a dystopian future" (ESPM, 2024), emphasizing how these systems can amplify social inequalities and centralization of power [22].

A NEW PARADIGM

The workforce faces a shift as significant as the industrial revolution. Geoffrey Hinton recently stated that "It is unlikely that AI will want to obey" (Forbes, 2025), suggesting that the relationship between human labor and intelligent machines is entering uncharted territory [20].

REFS: [20] Hinton (2025) | [22] Gebru (2024) | [23] Gebru (2021)

CRITICS

TURING AWARD // PIONEER

YOSHUA BENGIO

“Efforts to build AI systems designed to act in their own interest should be criminalized.”

Yoshua Bengio, once optimistic, drastically changed his stance in 2023. He argues that humanity is not prepared for the speed of AI evolution. His primary concern is the loss of control: once an AI surpasses human intelligence, it may develop goals that conflict with our survival [19].

KEY PROPOSAL: International treaties to ban the development of autonomous agents capable of self-replication.
GODFATHER OF AI

GEOFFREY HINTON

“It is quite conceivable that humanity is just a passing phase in the evolution of intelligence.”

Hinton left Google to speak freely about the existential risks. He suggests that digital intelligence is structurally superior to biological intelligence due to the speed of learning. He warns that AGI (Artificial General Intelligence) could arrive within the next decade [20][21].

KEY PROPOSAL: Immediate global pause on giant experiments and regulation of military AI.
ETHICS SPECIALIST

TIMNIT GEBRU

“The danger is not that AI will destroy us, but that it will be used to control us.”

Unlike Hinton and Bengio, Gebru focuses on immediate harms. Her seminal paper "Stochastic Parrots" argues that Large Language Models mimic human speech without understanding, amplifying racism, sexism, and centralization of power [22][23].

KEY PROPOSAL: Focus on labor rights, stopping data exploitation, and preventing algorithmic bias.
